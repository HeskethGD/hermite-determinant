\begin{center}
{\bf Excerpt from a letter by M. Ch. Hermite addressed to M. L. Fuchs}
\end{center}

\hrule

\noindent Let
\begin{equation*}
Z(x) \;=\; \frac{H'(x)}{H(x)} ,
\end{equation*}
and, with the help of this function, one can represent every elliptic function, having periods \(2K\) and \(2iK'\), by a formula entirely analogous to that of a rational fraction decomposed into simple fractions, namely:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
F(x) \;=\; \text{const.} &\;+\; A\,Z(x-a) \;+\; A_1\,D_x Z(x-a) \;+\; A_2\,D_x^2 Z(x-a) \;+\; \cdots
\\
&\;+\; B\,Z(x-b) \;+\; B_1\,D_x Z(x-b) \;+\; B_2\,D_x^2 Z(x-b) \;+\; \cdots
\\[-6pt]
&\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad \vdots \qquad \vdots \qquad \vdots \qquad \vdots \qquad \vdots \qquad \vdots
\\[-6pt]
&\;+\; L\,Z(x-\ell) \;+\; L_1\,D_x Z(x-\ell) \;+\; L_2\,D_x^2 Z(x-\ell) \;+\; \cdots
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where the constants \(A,\,B,\,\ldots,\,L\) are essentially required to satisfy the condition

\begin{equation}
A \;+\; B \;+\; \cdots \;+\; L \;=\; 0 .
\end{equation}

This expression, which I have used in many circumstances, I now employ in order to seek the coordinates of a planar cubic surface in explicit form in terms of a parameter. I set:

\begin{align}
x &= x_0 \;+\; A\,Z(t-a) \;+\; B\,Z(t-b) \;+\; C\,Z(t-c), \\
y &= y_0 \;+\; A'\,Z(t-a) \;+\; B'\,Z(t-b) \;+\; C'\,Z(t-c),
\end{align}

with the conditions
\begin{equation}
A \;+\; B \;+\; C \;=\; 0,\qquad
A' \;+\; B' \;+\; C' \;=\; 0,
\end{equation}
so that the coordinates \(x\) and \(y\) will be linear functions of the two differences
\(Z(t-a)-Z(t-c)\) and \(Z(t-b)-Z(t-c)\).
That being so, I remark that \(x^2\), \(xy\), \(y^2\) being doubly periodic uniform functions with periods \(2K\) and \(2iK'\), are expressed linearly, on the one hand by these two differences, and on the other hand by the derivatives \(D_tZ(t-a)\), \(D_tZ(t-b)\), \(D_tZ(t-c)\).
And likewise, if one considers \(x^3,\; x^2y,\; xy^2,\; y^3\), it results from the general formula that one will have only the second derivatives:
\(D_t^2 Z(t-a),\,D_t^2 Z(t-b),\,D_t^2 Z(t-c)\) to be joined to the first derivatives and to the previous terms.

\begin{center}
\smallskip
And the same pattern applies as one proceeds.
\end{center}

% -- PAGE_BREAK --

The various homogeneous functions of $x$ and $y$, up to degree $\mu$, whose number is $2+3+\cdots+\mu+1 = \frac{1}{2}(\mu^2+3\mu)$, and let $m$ denote the number of the transcendents. All these doubly periodic functions are expressible linearly by the differences $Z(t-a)-Z(t-b)$, $Z(t-b)-Z(t-c)$, $\ldots$, in number $m-1$, and then by the derivatives up to the order $\mu-1$ of the quantities $Z(t-a)$, that is, in all by $m-1+m(\mu-1)$ functions. In order to be able to perform the elimination of these functions, I impose the condition:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{2}(\mu^{2}+3\mu) = m + m(\mu-1) = m\,\mu
\end{equation}
which gives me \(\mu = 2m-3\), so that I reach by this route an order \(2m-3\), instead of obtaining the order \(m\). The procedure that succeeds in the case \(m=3\) therefore, in general, yields a degree too high, and I have completely given up, as a method of elimination. But the existence, at least, of an equation of this degree \(m\) is very easy to prove. Consider for this the arbitrary line \(ax+by+\gamma=0\), whose intersection points with the curve will be obtained by determining \(t\) from the equation:
\begin{equation*}
ax+by+\gamma+\left(Aa+A' b\right)Z(t-a)+\left(Ba+B' b\right)Z(t-b)+\cdots = 0.
\end{equation*}
The first member of this equation is a doubly periodic function which becomes infinite for the \(m\) values of \(t\) (inside the rectangle of periods \(2K\) and \(2K'\)); the curve cannot be cut by any straight line except at \(m\) points, hence it is of order \(m\). This same reasoning, applied to the polar, whose coordinates are
\begin{equation*}
X = -\,\frac{y'}{xy'-x'y}, \qquad Y = \frac{x'}{xy'-x'y},
\end{equation*}
determines its degree. And indeed the intersections of this second curve with the line \(\alpha X+\beta Y+\gamma=0\) are given by the equation
\begin{equation*}
-\alpha y' + \beta x' + \gamma\,(xy'-yx') = 0,
\end{equation*}
and you see that its first member is again a function doubly periodic, admitting the infinite double points \(t=a,b,\dots,l\); hence there are \(2m\) roots and therefore \(2m\) intersection points. Knowing the order of the polar of Clebsch, \(\delta=2m\), the number \(d\) of double points of these curves follows immediately, as a consequence of the relation
\begin{equation}
2d+\delta = m(m-1).
\end{equation}
Paris, 29 June 1876.

\textbf{P.\,S.} The determination of the inflection points of the plane cubic, and of the stationary points of the quadric in space, depend on the following equations:
\begin{equation*}
\left|\begin{array}{cc}
Z'(t-a)-Z'(t-c) & Z'(t-b)-Z'(t-c) \\
Z''(t-a)-Z''(t-c) & Z''(t-b)-Z''(t-c)
\end{array}\right| = 0
\end{equation*}
and:
\begin{equation*}
\left|\begin{array}{ccc}
Z'(t-a)-Z'(t-d) & Z'(t-b)-Z'(t-d) & Z'(t-c)-Z'(t-d) \\
Z''(t-a)-Z''(t-d) & Z''(t-b)-Z''(t-d) & Z''(t-c)-Z''(t-d) \\
Z'''(t-a)-Z'''(t-d) & Z'''(t-b)-Z'''(t-d) & Z'''(t-c)-Z'''(t-d)
\end{array}\right| = 0.
\end{equation*}

I have proposed to calculate the determinants which form the first members, and I have found the following expressions. For brevity, let
\begin{equation*}
\Phi(a,b,c) = H(a-b)\,H(a-c)\,H(b-c),
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
\Phi(a,b,c,d) = H(a-b)\,H(a-c)\,H(a-d)\,H(b-c)\,H(b-d)\,H(c-d),
\end{equation*}
the first determinant is:
\begin{equation*}
H'(0)^5\,\frac{\Phi(a,b,c)\,H(3t-a-b-c)}{[H(t-a)\,H(t-b)\,H(t-c)]^3},
\end{equation*}
and the second:
\begin{equation*}
H'(0)^9\,\frac{\Phi(a,b,c,d)\,H(4t-a-b-c-d)}{[H(t-a)\,H(t-b)\,H(t-c)\,H(t-d)]^4}.
\end{equation*}

The beautiful results discovered by Clebsch are the consequence of these expressions, which led me to consider in general the determinant with $n-1$ columns:
\begin{equation*}
\left|\begin{array}{cccc}
Z'(t-a)-Z'(t-l) & Z'(t-b)-Z'(t-l) & \cdots & Z'(t-k)-Z'(t-l) \\
Z''(t-a)-Z''(t-l) & Z''(t-b)-Z''(t-l) & \cdots & Z''(t-k)-Z''(t-l) \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
Z^{(n-1)}(t-a)-Z^{(n-1)}(t-l) & Z^{(n-1)}(t-b)-Z^{(n-1)}(t-l) & \cdots & Z^{(n-1)}(t-k)-Z^{(n-1)}(t-l)
\end{array}\right| = 0.
\end{equation*}

% -- PAGE_BREAK --
where $a, b, \ldots, k, l$ are $n$ constants. If one defines as before:
\begin{equation*}
\Phi(a, b, \dots, k, l) = H(a-b)\,H(a-c)\cdots H(a-l)\; H(b-c)\cdots H(b-l)\;\cdots\; H(k-l)
\end{equation*}
one finds that it has the value:
\begin{equation*}
\mu\, H'(0)^{\frac{1}{2}(n-1)(n+2)}\, \frac{\Phi(a, b, \dots, k, l)\, H(nt - a - b - \cdots - l)}{[H(t-a)\, H(t-b) \cdots H(t-l)]^{n}}
\end{equation*}
$\mu$ denoting a numerical factor.
Paris, December 29, 1876.
